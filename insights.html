Are you innovating to improve your product portfolio and keep up with the competition? Then it makes sense to use all the tax breaks available to you as the corporate tax rises. The patent box scheme is one of the most attractive tax incentives in the UK and is now closely tied to R&D tax incentives: you can get tax incentives for the development of your intellectual property and a lower tax rate on profits from its use.

If you haven't chosen a patent box scheme yet, it's time to check how it can benefit your business in 2023 and beyond.

Benefits

Profits eligible for the patent box exemption are effectively taxed at just 10%, up from a base corporate tax rate of 25% from 2023. And you do not need to patent a completely new product, even a small holistic patent on one of your products will give you access to all the benefits of the patent box regime.

The main criteria for access to this valuable relief are as follows:

  • You own or hold an exclusive license to an EU or UK patent;
  • The company that owns the patents also carried out the R&D that led to their creation; and
  • You can track and trace the relationship between research and development, patents and their revenue.
  • Apart from that, the calculation of the patent box is pretty much of a formulaic nature, so once you set up the process of collecting information, it can work without interruption for many years.

It is also possible to structure the group so that the IP company is shielded from major trading activities (thereby protecting it in the event of bankruptcy), but still entitled to an effective 10% corporate tax rate on license income. receives from the group's sales organizations.

Are you meeting your responsibility to report payment methods and performance requirements?

The UK government recognizes that thousands of organizations carry significant administrative and financial burdens because they are not paid on time. Consequently, all major UK companies are now required to publicly disclose their payment practices, policies and performance.

Learn more

The UK government recognizes that thousands of organizations carry significant administrative and financial burdens because they are not paid on time. Consequently, all major UK companies are now required to publicly disclose their payment practices, policies and performance.

Although the rules were introduced in 2017, many organizations and charities still fail to comply with them. The requirements are being met and have received a lot of attention from various organizations over the past twelve months.

Who's affected?

The requirements apply to charitable companies (including limited liability companies with shares and guarantees) and the public interest that meet two of the following criteria:

Revenue £ 36m
Total assets £ 18m
Employees £ 250m

What does it mean?

A charity meeting the above criteria may be required to publish a report on the government portal BEIS.
There are usually two reporting periods in a standard fiscal year, each spanning six months of the respective fiscal year. For non-standard fiscal years, such as shortened or extended periods, the reporting requirements and therefore the corresponding timing may differ. The required information must be published within 30 days after the end of the reporting period.
The rules state that if an organization does not publish its report or provides false information in the report, it is considered a criminal offense and the company and directors will be fined if convicted under the summary procedure. However, BIES commented that it “will generally seek to induce businesses to comply with the reporting requirement before prosecutions are taken.”

Action

Trustees of charities that meet the criteria or are truly close to the thresholds should make sure they are familiar with the requirements and make sure their charities are meeting their
reporting responsibilities. Trustees should also review the reported performance and consider whether their charitable activities are performing at an acceptable level, how their reports can be viewed by third parties and stakeholders, and whether their payment policies and procedures are reliable.
If any of the above questions raise any questions, BDO is well placed to provide support and advice on these rules and broader working capital issues.

Working outside wages - ir35 in the private sector

The new IR35 rules came into effect on April 6, 2021, and all public sector enterprises, as well as medium and large private enterprises, are now responsible for determining the employment status of their workers. This includes some charities and third sector organizations...

Learn more

The new IR35 rules came into effect on April 6, 2021, and all public sector enterprises, as well as medium and large private enterprises, are now responsible for determining the employment status of their workers. This includes some charities and third sector organizations...

Which companies are affected by IR35 in the private sector?

The new rules are similar to those used for the IR35 public sector reforms in 2017, but go further in a number of respects (and these extensions also apply to public sector enterprises from April 2021). However, private sector rules will only apply to medium and large enterprises, with the definition of small businesses exempt from taxation based largely on the definition of small company in the Companies Act 2006. At the request of an employee (or an organization that concludes a contract with an end customer), any organization must confirm within 45 days whether it is considered "small" in accordance with these rules

How will IR35 affect your business?

The changes affect organizations that engage workers through Personal Service Companies (PSCs) or any other intermediary such as a partnership or LLP. This is because the new rules will require the employing organization (client) to assess whether the intermediary rules (IR35) apply to contracts it enters into with any PSC employed directly or through third parties.
If the customer determines that IR35 applies, the individual or legal entity paying the PSC (payer of the commission) must apply the PAYE and NIC deductions to employee fees. In other words, they must treat the employee as a prospective employee for tax purposes. In addition, the payer should also consider the employer's NIC and possibly a tuition fee.

These changes can lead to an increase in the operating costs of your business:

+ Directly as a result of paying a commission

+ Indirectly, as organizations down the supply chain seek to increase their fees to mitigate potential increases in their tax and NIC liabilities.

The government's response to the implementation review confirmed that it will be cautious about penalties for errors in the first year of the new rules. However, businesses need to take their new commitments seriously to avoid being listed as HMRC's risks for years to come.

If you do need to assess your situation, it is important to define your PSC usage profile and perhaps your broader portfolio of non-payroll employees both now and as part of any new business model you adopt. Your key stakeholders will need to have a clear understanding of:

+ How many PSCs do you use?

+ Which parts of your business use them?

+ How are they used?

+ How important they are to different parts of the business?

This portfolio analysis will clarify which commercial contracts are awarded and whether they are fit for purpose. Based on our experience in helping organizations to date, it is often appropriate to introduce updated contracts that meet the needs of the organization in the event that IR35 will apply to a specific arrangement.
Once all PSC use has been identified, processes must be put in place to ensure that the IR35 assessment is completed prior to employee interaction provided through the PSC. It is clear that it is very important to determine which part of the business will be responsible for the IR35 verification process and to consider what level of knowledge exists in the business. You may need to train and support the appropriate staff.

What is the minimum wage?

The national minimum wage starts at £ 4.30 per hour for those working as an apprentice.
If you are under 18, the minimum amount you can receive is £ 4.62 per hour, rising to £ 6.56 for those ages 18-20 and £ 8.36 for those ages 21 to 22 years old.
Individuals aged 23 and over earn the national subsistence minimum wage and are eligible for a minimum of £ 8.91 per hour.

Hourly rates currently:

£ 8.91 – Age 23 and Over (National Living Wage)
£ 8.36 – Age 21-22
£ 6.56 – Ages 18-20
£ 4.62 – under 18
£ 4.30 – Apprentice
Workers are entitled to correct wages, even if they are working on a temporary basis, working part-time or on a temporary basis, or if they have not yet received training.

People who do not have to pay a minimum amount include self-employed and self-employed businesses, company directors and volunteers.

How to check if you have received underpaid payments?

To make sure you're underpaid, you need to look at your payrolls and compare them to the minimum rates.
If you think you are not being paid enough, you should first talk to your employer to give them a chance to remedy the situation.
If this does not resolve the issue, you can file a complaint with HMRC through the Gov.uk website.
They then investigate your complaint, and if you are underpaid, you will refund the money owed.

Accounting giant Ernst & Young was fined £ 3.5 million and severely reprimanded for failing to audit the Scottish shipping company Stagecoach. The Financial Reporting Board (FRC) also fined Mark Harvey, former Scottish chief accountant of the Big Four, £ 100,000 in connection with the audit of the bus giant's accounts in Perth for the year ended April 29, 2017.

The ruling is likely to bring renewed attention to the quality of audit work performed by the UK's top accountants following the publication of the latest results of the FRC audit quality audit in July. Sir John Thompson, Chief Executive Officer of FRC, concluded that "significant changes still need to occur to substantially improve audit quality."

EY stated that it "did not meet the standards we set for ourselves."

The FRC said EY's deficiencies in Stagecoach relate to three specific areas, including an onerous contract clause regarding the East Coast rail franchise that the company operated with Virgin Trains from 2014 until its re-nationalization in 2018.

Former UK Transport Secretary Chris Grayling got the franchise back after Stagecoach reported heavy contract losses.

The FRC stated that the sanctions imposed on EY in connection with the Stagecoach audit are also linked to certain retirement plan obligations and provisions for insurance claims related to accidents.

“All three audit areas concerned material residues and were identified by respondents as areas of significant risk requiring increased auditor attention,” the FRC said in a statement.

The supervisory body ruled that the “most serious deficiencies” in the audit of EY Stagecoach “related to the lack of sufficient appraisal and performance problems by the relevant management experts and respondents, and the consequent lack of proper management challenging the significant underlying assumptions. financial reporting ".

“Although it is not alleged that the financial statements were actually misstated, in several significant cases respondents were unable to obtain sufficient appropriate audit evidence and did not display sufficient professional skepticism in conducting the audit,” the FRC said in a statement.

“In addition, the content and volume of audit documentation that respondents were required to prepare was of low quality, which did not reflect the full scope of procedures and judgments made.”

But the FRC added: "The violation of the relevant requirements was not willful, dishonest, willful or reckless."

The FRC stated that EY has taken steps to identify the causes of audit errors related to one year of audit and has taken steps to address those issues. He noted that EY "provided a good level of cooperation during the investigation."

Claudia Mortimore, Deputy Executive Adviser of FRC, said: “The audit shortcomings in this case were widespread and related to a number of fundamental auditing standards, including the requirement to obtain sufficient appropriate audit evidence, adequate expert judgment, exercise of sufficient professional skepticism and challenge. management and prepare appropriate audit documentation.

"The sanctions imposed reflect the severity of the violations and are intended to improve the quality of future audits."

The penalties imposed on EY were reduced to £ 2.2m, 10% less for mitigating factors and 30% for admission and early removal. Mr. Harvey, who was also severely reprimanded, received a 30% penalty cut to £ 70,000 for hospitalization and early termination. He left EY last summer to become Arnold Clark's CFO and was replaced by Ellie Scott.

EY said in a statement: “Auditing builds confidence and confidence in the economy, and conducting high quality audits is an absolute priority for us. Unfortunately, in this case, we did not reach the standards that we set for ourselves, and the standards that FRC and society expect of us.

“We have worked with FRC throughout the investigation, take their findings very seriously and have worked hard to fix the issues identified. The FRC review of our most recent end-2020 audit of the company did not draw any conclusions.
